## Bibliographic information

Harvard Business Review. 2026. Contributor Guidelines for Harvard Business Review Authors. Last updated June 10, 2026. Official URL: `https://hbr.org/guidelines-for-authors`.

## Source tier

Tier A: official guidance or editorial. This is an official publisher page for prospective HBR contributors.

## Authority rationale

The page is authoritative for HBR's stated submission and editorial expectations. It is not a universal scholarly standard or evidence that any management claim is empirically valid.

## Inclusion rationale

The source directly states how one major practitioner outlet evaluates management ideas for expertise, evidence, originality, usefulness, writing quality, and audience fit.

## Problem addressed

The page addresses how contributors should propose and develop useful management content for HBR's senior-leader audience. It also explains why promising submissions can still be declined during a non-guaranteed editorial process.

## Central contribution

HBR organizes its guidance around five publication qualities and seven pitch questions. Together they ask a contributor to state the main idea, explain its importance and distinctiveness, identify its practical use and basis of authority, and supply research, examples, or experience that support it. The page also sets expectations for originality, attribution, conflicts disclosure, and human accountability for generative-AI-assisted work.

## Supported framing principles

The reviewed guidance supports making a central message explicit, writing for a defined senior-leader audience, explaining practical applicability, distinguishing the idea from familiar coverage, and backing insights or recommendations with traceable research or examples. It also supports clear writing and inclusive applications and examples as publisher-specific communication criteria.

## What it does not establish

The page does not establish a research design, causal-identification standard, model-validation procedure, mechanism test, or universal definition of importance. It does not show that an article satisfying HBR's criteria is scientifically valid or suitable for a scholarly journal.

## Managerial-framing implications

For practitioner-facing framing, the guidance supports identifying the senior-leader audience, stating the message and practical use, and explaining why the idea matters now. Its support for `MGR-001` is partial and outlet-specific: it motivates audience and application clarity, while the full requirement to center a consequential choice owned by a named actor also depends on other evidence and model-grounded analysis.

## Scholarly-positioning implications

The page asks contributors to explain what is new, distinctive, or counterintuitive and how an idea builds on prior work. Those prompts can help clarify positioning, but they do not define or validate a scholarly theoretical contribution.

## Candidate Skill rules

The manifest assigns `MGR-001` and `EVD-001`. The page gives bounded support to `MGR-001` through its senior-leader audience and application requirements. It directly supports the evidentiary core of `EVD-001` by requiring contributors to demonstrate the validity of insights and recommendations through supporting research or relevant examples. Neither assignment turns HBR guidance into a universal research standard.

## Anti-patterns

The page cautions against an unclear central message, weak audience fit, familiar prescriptions without a distinctive insight, unsupported recommendations, promotional content, improper attribution, undisclosed relevant financial relationships, and treating generative AI as a substitute for human accountability.
