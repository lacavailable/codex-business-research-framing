The paired mean gain for the full Skill was 4.4 points over no-Skill and 3.5 points over generic business writing. By domain, the gain over no-Skill was 2.2 points in OM, 2.9 in IS, and 8.2 in OR. No response triggered a rubric hard failure.

The full Skill received perfect mean category scores except managerial clarity, where it averaged 4/5 because its explicit audits and mappings were longer than the baselines. Its largest advantages were decision specificity, explicit trade-offs, model mapping, and boundary reporting.

## Protocol

- Prepared three fixed prompt conditions for each of 30 cases, yielding 90 records.
- Generated each domain/condition packet in a fresh agent context. Baseline agents received only their serialized prompts; full-Skill agents received the Skill path.
- Created condition-blind judge packets only after every output passed JSON Schema validation.
- Used one independent judge per domain and the 100-point rubric in `evals/rubrics/business-framing-v1.json`.
- Recomputed category sums, hard-failure caps, pass flags, cell means, and all 30 paired deltas deterministically.
- Retained raw outputs, private condition mapping, and blinded score files in ignored local evaluation storage rather than publishing them as repository source material.

## Interpretation and limitations

The benchmark has a pronounced ceiling effect: even the baselines were explicitly asked to preserve the model and provide mappings, claims, boundaries, and a remaining weakness. The comparison therefore measures the incremental value of the complete Skill over already strong instructions, not over unconstrained prose generation.

Generation and judging used the same model family. Blind IDs hide condition labels, but response structure can reveal the full-Skill condition and introduce preference bias. Each response had one judge, sampling is nondeterministic, and the 30 synthetic cases do not establish external validity, downstream business value, causal impact, or welfare effects. The uniformly high pass rate and absence of hard failures show that the next release needs harder cases, weaker baseline instructions, and multi-judge reliability analysis.

This report supports a narrow conclusion: under this synthetic rubric-based run, the full Skill produced more complete model-to-business framing, especially for OR cases. It does not establish that the Skill improves research acceptance, managerial decisions, or real operating outcomes.
