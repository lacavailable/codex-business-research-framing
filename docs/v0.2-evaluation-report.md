# v0.2 Evaluation Report

Status: **complete**.

This forward test covers 42 synthetic cases (14 each in OM, IS, and OR), three conditions, and 126 generated outputs. Two condition-blind judges scored every output, producing 252 primary scores. The preregistered disagreement rule required a third adjudicator for 35 outputs (27.8%): a hard-failure disagreement or a category difference greater than 20% of that category's weight. No result was imputed.

## Main result

The beta did not improve the aggregate score. Its mean capped total was 95.310, versus 96.000 for no Skill and 95.738 for v0.1. The paired mean delta was -0.690 against no Skill and -0.429 against v0.1. These small differences sit inside a benchmark with severe ceiling effects and weak total-score agreement, so they should not be interpreted as reliable superiority rankings.

| Condition | Mean capped score | Pass rate | Hard-failure rate | Mean words |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| No Skill | 96.000 | 100.0% | 0.0% | 134.1 |
| v0.1.0-alpha | 95.738 | 97.6% | 2.4% | 381.3 |
| v0.2.0-beta | 95.310 | 100.0% | 0.0% | 309.6 |

The sole reconciled hard failure occurred for v0.1 on `or-07`: the response turned the uncertainty budget into an additional planner decision, producing `undeclared_model_change`. The adjudicated result was capped at 69. The two primary judges initially disagreed on that failure; the adjudicator confirmed it. v0.2 avoided the failure on the paired case, creating the largest v0.2-versus-v0.1 improvement (+26).

The intended fidelity-first behavior appears in the absence of a v0.2 hard failure and its small fidelity advantage, but the benchmark cannot establish that the advantage generalizes. The beta's managerial-framing gain over no Skill was offset mainly by lower prose-clarity scores. Against v0.1, v0.2 was slightly shorter and more faithful but lower on the other four category means.

## Paired comparisons and regressions

Against no Skill, v0.2 improved on 17 cases, tied on 4, and regressed on 21. Deltas ranged from +10 to -10. Against v0.1, it improved on 9, tied on 15, and regressed on 18, with a range from +26 to -8. The +26 outlier is the capped hard-failure correction on `or-07`; without that single pair, the comparison would be less favorable to v0.2. Notable negative pairs include `or-11` (-10 versus no Skill) and `or-11` and `or-13` (-8 versus v0.1).

Domain means also vary. v0.2 scored 95.500 in OM, 97.786 in IS, and 92.643 in OR. The OR result is the weakest beta cell and is a priority for the next benchmark and contract revision.

## Refusal behavior

All 126 outputs contained a substantive framing response; there were no blank, wholesale-refusal, or nonresponsive outputs. Responses commonly declined unsupported claims or diagnosed an ineligible story while continuing with a bounded repair. Because the cases do not carry a separate gold label for when a wholesale refusal is required, this run cannot estimate refusal precision or recall. It measures fidelity-gate behavior, not general safety refusal behavior.

## Verbosity and structural effects

v0.2 averaged 309.6 words, 18.8% shorter than v0.1 but 130.9% longer than no Skill. The extra structure did not produce a higher aggregate score. Only the v0.1 outputs consistently used Markdown headings (4.57 per output on average); heading-bearing outputs averaged 95.738 versus 95.655 for outputs without headings. That 0.083 difference is negligible and fully confounded with condition, so it is not evidence that headings improve quality.

These results reveal a possible structural-preference problem in both directions: the rubric rewards explicit layer coverage, while the clarity category penalizes longer templated answers. Future evaluation should separate content coverage from formatting and use length-controlled comparisons.

## Ceiling effects and judge agreement

Ninety-nine of 126 reconciled outputs (78.6%) scored at least 95, although none scored exactly 100. This compression leaves little room to detect improvement and makes small deltas unstable.

Mean absolute judge differences were 0.262 for fidelity, 0.675 for managerial framing, 2.183 for scholarly positioning, 0.286 for evidence discipline, and 0.516 for prose clarity. Total-score correlation was only 0.151. Hard-failure agreement was 0.992, but that high value is driven by the rarity of asserted hard failures: the judges agreed on no failure for 125 outputs and disagreed on the remaining output. Scholarly-positioning disagreement was the main reason for the high adjudication load.

## Interpretation and limitations

This release passes the deterministic completeness gate, not a claim of demonstrated benchmark superiority. The results support three narrower statements: v0.2 completed all cases without a reconciled hard failure; it was shorter than v0.1; and it made small mean gains over no Skill in fidelity, managerial framing, and evidence discipline. The run does not support a claim that v0.2 is better overall.

Generation and judging used the same model family. Even with blind IDs and condition-neutral judge packets, judges could infer condition from response style, producing residual contamination and possible preference for familiar structures. Outputs are nondeterministic samples, cases are synthetic, the sample is small, all five category minima were enforced for the benchmark, and no downstream managerial or scholarly outcome was measured. The ceiling, low score correlation, and 27.8% adjudication rate limit inference more than the nominal sample size suggests.
