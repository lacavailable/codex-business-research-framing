# IS Framing Playbook

Use this playbook for platforms, algorithms, digital intermediaries, information design, and strategic users.

## Information and signals

Distinguish private type, observable action, noisy signal, inferred score, and verified fact. Place each on the decision timeline. Never let a screening or moderation policy condition on information the platform cannot observe at that point.

## Platform governance and digital rules

Identify who sets the rule, who responds, how compliance or evasion occurs, and which platform objective is modeled. Treat a rule as a commitment only if timing and enforcement support commitment.

## Screening, review, and moderation

Separate automated scoring, intermediary review, final decision, and appeal. For resource-constrained review, state the review budget and which cases consume it. Distinguish false positives, false negatives, refusal, deferral, and removal when the model does.

## Strategic users and externalities

Describe beliefs, incentives, and available actions without assuming unmodeled sophistication. For benign and malicious users, state whether type is latent, drawn, learned, or strategically selected. Identify network or content externalities and whose payoff includes them.

## LLM-mediated interaction

For reply, review, and refusal settings, identify whether an LLM generates, recommends, screens, or executes. State who reviews what, when refusal occurs, and whether the system observes intent or only signals. Do not equate model refusal with verified safety or model approval with factual correctness.

## Privacy and fairness

Claim privacy or fairness improvement only when a defined measure is modeled or evaluated. Name the unit and affected group. A constraint, proxy, or regularizer is not automatically a real-world guarantee.

## IS trade-off prompts

- Information quality versus privacy or collection cost.
- Access and participation versus abuse or review burden.
- Automation speed versus error, accountability, or human capacity.
- Screening strictness versus exclusion of benign users.
- Personalization versus fairness, privacy, or manipulation risk.

Expose platform, user, and third-party objectives separately. State boundaries around user adaptation, measurement error, enforcement, spillovers, and institutional context.
