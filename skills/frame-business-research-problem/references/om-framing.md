# OM Framing Playbook

Use this playbook for operating systems, service systems, supply chains, and marketplace operations.

## Capacity and resource allocation

Name the scarce resource, unit, planning horizon, and owner. Distinguish installed capacity, scheduled capacity, and realized availability. Explain whether unused capacity is perishable and whether allocation decisions are reversible.

## Inventory and fulfillment

Separate stocking, allocation, replenishment, fulfillment, and routing decisions. State when demand arrives, whether backlogging or lost sales is allowed, and what inventory information is observed. Avoid treating realized fulfillment as a controllable decision when it follows from demand and stock.

## Demand uncertainty and substitution

Specify what is known about demand at decision time. For substitution, identify the unavailable or omitted option, the customer's choice set, and the response rule. Do not add substitution to an independent-demand model merely to make the story vivid.

## Congestion

Identify arrivals, service resources, queue discipline, service-time uncertainty, and congestion consequences. Distinguish steady-state approximations from transient operations. Do not imply that managers directly choose waiting times when they choose staffing or priority rules.

## Assortment and display

For quick-commerce or online display settings, distinguish assortment availability from display prominence and inventory availability. State capacity in the modeled unit: slots, shelf space, attention, picks, or another resource. If using a Markov-chain choice model, preserve the modeled transition and absorption interpretation; do not claim individual path observability unless supplied.

## OM trade-off prompts

- Breadth of assortment versus capacity or operational complexity.
- Responsiveness versus staffing, inventory, or expediting cost.
- Pooling efficiency versus service differentiation.
- Utilization versus congestion or resilience.
- Immediate fulfillment versus protecting inventory for later demand.

Tie each trade-off to an objective term, constraint, or mechanism. State boundary conditions such as stable demand, known substitution parameters, fixed capacity, or omitted strategic behavior.
